Induction as Initiality

Why induction holds, why J is exactly the right principle (generic but no junk), and why K is junk. A side-quest reference, grounded in the category theory you already know.

The one idea An inductive type is the initial algebra of a functor. Its recursor is the unique map out of the initial object, and its induction principle is the dependent form of that same universal property. "Induction holds" and "the eliminator is exactly right" are both just "this is the initial object."

1. Inductive type = initial algebra

Bundle a type's constructors into one map. For ℕ, the constructors zero and suc bundle into [zero, suc] : 1 + ℕ → ℕ. So ℕ is an algebra for the functor F(X) = 1 + X. Being initial means: for every other algebra α : F(A) → A, there is exactly one homomorphism out of ℕ.

      F(ℕ) ──F(h)──▶ F(A)
       │              │
 [zero,suc]          α│              h : ℕ → A  exists and is UNIQUE
       ▼              ▼                 = the recursor / fold
       ℕ ────h──────▶ A

That unique h is recursion: you specify the algebra A (a value for zero, a step for suc) and initiality hands you the map. Induction is the dependent version: the same universal property in the category of dependent algebras (sections instead of maps).

2. J is that idea, for one constructor

The identity family a = − is the inductive type with a single constructor refl. Apply the schema "map out by handling each constructor" and you get J: handle the one case, refl.

NAT    two constructors, suc RECURSES          ->  base + STEP
a = y  one constructor, refl does NOT recurse  ->  base only (that is J)

A step case appears exactly when a constructor is recursive. suc takes a ℕ (recursive); refl takes no path (not). So J needing only the refl case is not special pleading, it is the general rule with one constructor. See Lesson 5.

3. Why J is generic but has no junk

"Exactly right" splits into the two halves of a universal property:

  • Generic (existence). J defines a map out of = for any motive. Nothing is out of reach.
  • No junk (uniqueness). The map J returns is the unique one extending your refl-choice, and that uniqueness is provable from J. You pick where refl goes; everything else is determined, not invented.
The sharp statement: fundamental theorem of identity types A family R with a point r : R a satisfies J's induction principle if and only if R y ≃ (a = y) for all y. So "satisfies J" characterizes the identity type up to equivalence. Anything with J's power is the identity type: J is neither too weak (it defines everything) nor too strong (it is satisfied by the genuine path type). (Rijke, Introduction to Homotopy Type Theory.)

4. J vs K: how we know the junk line

The external check is soundness against models. J survives every model where types are genuinely spaces; K, the stronger cousin that pins the endpoint (see Lesson 5), does not.

principletypes are setsgroupoids∞-groupoids / cubical
J✓✓✓   sound wherever paths are real
K (UIP)✓✗✗   only if the tower is trivial

The Hofmann–Streicher groupoid model is the proof: J survives it, K dies in it. So K is the concrete example of an eliminator with junk. It asserts more than refl-being-the-only-constructor licenses, and that surplus is exactly UIP.

The twist worth keeping. More power in the eliminator does not extend the theory, it shrinks it: K forces every type to be a set. J's restraint is what allows the tall tower (groupoids, the circle, univalence). So J is not "less generic" than K; K is more restrictive on which types may exist. Genericity comes from claiming the minimum.

Is there anything more generic than J?

  • Upward: yes. J is one instance of the single schema "the eliminator of an inductive type handles each constructor," i.e. initiality of inductive types (this whole page). That is the genuinely-more-generic thing, and J is it, specialized to refl.
  • Stronger-for-=: no (sound one). J already pins = down up to equivalence, so no sound principle can determine more. K only looks stronger; it is unsound for real paths.
