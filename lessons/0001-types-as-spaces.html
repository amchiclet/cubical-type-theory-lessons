Cubical Type Theory · Lesson 1 Glossary

Types are Spaces

The one reframe that makes all of HoTT (and cubical) readable: an equality proof is a path, and a type is the space those paths live in.

The win for this lesson After this you can read the sentence "types are ∞-groupoids and equalities are paths" and unpack every word. That sentence is the lens for everything else: univalence, transport, the cubical interval. Get it now and the rest is variations on it.

Start from what you already know

In ordinary dependent type theory you already have the identity type. For a b : A there is a type a =A b (often written IdA(a,b)), and it has one constructor, refl : a =A a.

The habit from ordinary type theory is to treat a =A b as a yes/no thing: either it is inhabited (a equals b) or it is not. HoTT makes exactly one move, and everything follows from it:

The move Take the identity type seriously as a type. It can have many different inhabitants, and those inhabitants can themselves be related by further identity types. Stop reading a =A b as a fact. Read it as a space of ways that a and b are equal.

The dictionary

Once you make that move, a translation appears between type theory and geometry. This table is the heart of the lesson. Everything in the course is you learning to read fluently in both columns at once.

Type theoryGeometry / space
a type Aa space
an element a : Aa point in the space
a proof p : a =A ba path from point a to point b
refl : a =A athe path that stays put (constant path)
a proof a =A b is impossibleno path connects a and b (different components)
a proof r : p =(a=b) qa path between the two paths p and q

The tower does not stop

Here is the part that feels strange at first, then becomes the whole point. Since a =A b is itself a type, and it has two elements p and q, we can ask about its identity type: p =(a=b) q. That is a path between paths. And it too is a type, so it has its own paths, and so on without end.

So a single type quietly contains an infinite tower:

  • points (its elements),
  • paths between points,
  • paths between paths,
  • paths between paths between paths, forever.

A path between paths is what a topologist calls a homotopy: a continuous way to slide one path onto the other. That is where the "homotopy" in Homotopy Type Theory comes from. The name is not decoration. The identity type is the space of paths, with all its higher structure.

Why "∞-groupoid" (this is where your category theory pays off)

Paths behave like the arrows of a groupoid, and you already know what that is: a category in which every arrow is invertible.

Groupoid lawPath operationProved by
identity arrowrefl : a = athe constant path
inversep⁻¹ : b = awalk the path backwards (symmetry)
compositionp · q : a = cconcatenate paths (transitivity)

So every type is at least a groupoid: points as objects, paths as invertible arrows. The catch, and the reason for the "∞", is that the groupoid laws (like p · refl = p, or associativity) do not hold on the nose. They hold up to a higher path. And those higher paths satisfy their own laws only up to even higher paths. A structure where the laws hold up to arrows, all the way up, is an ∞-groupoid.

The sentence, unpacked "Types are ∞-groupoids and equalities are paths" now reads as: every type is a space carrying points, paths, and higher paths without end; a proof that two things are equal is a path between them; and the algebra of those paths (compose, invert, identity) is the algebra of a groupoid, holding up to higher paths forever.
A fair objection: "but in the type theory I know, any two proofs of a = b are themselves equal. Isn't the tower trivial?" That extra assumption is called uniqueness of identity proofs (UIP), or axiom K. HoTT deliberately drops it. Without K the tower can be genuinely non-trivial, and that is what lets types model real spaces like the circle. When a type theory keeps K, every type is forced down to a "set" with a boring tower. We will make "how tall is the tower" precise next lesson.

Check yourself

Answer from memory before clicking. The click gives you immediate feedback.

In the space reading, what is an element p : a =A b?

A path from a to b. The identity type a =A b is the space of such paths, and p is one point of it.

Why does the phrase say "∞-groupoid" rather than just "groupoid"?

Paths compose, invert, and have identities, but the laws (unit, associativity) hold up to a higher path, whose laws hold up to still higher paths. That endless tower is the "∞".

What does dropping axiom K (uniqueness of identity proofs) buy you?

K collapses every higher path, making all towers boring. Dropping it lets a = b have several distinct, unequal proofs, which is what encodes genuine spatial structure (like the circle's loop).

A colleague who knows type theory asks: "What actually changes when I move from my type theory to HoTT?" Answer in three sentences.

We reinterpret the identity type a =A b as a space of paths rather than a yes/no fact, and we allow it to have many distinct inhabitants. Because that identity type is itself a type, it has its own paths, and we get an endless tower of paths between paths, which is exactly the structure of an ∞-groupoid (or a space). Concretely we drop uniqueness of identity proofs, so equality can carry real structure instead of always being trivial.
